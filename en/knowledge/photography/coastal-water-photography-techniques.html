Coastal and Water Photography: Shooting Techniques for Compelling Images

Master coastal photography techniques including ICM, long exposure, and silhouettes. Learn how to apply these methods to everyday subjects and create impactful monochrome images.

Photography Updated: 2026-07-20

Ichiro Murata · Photographer / SideKick Developer

Coastal areas and dunes offer exceptional creative potential. With waves, sand patterns, and light reflections working together, these locations transform dramatically based on your chosen shooting techniques. This guide covers practical methods for coastal photography and how to apply these same principles to everyday subjects with an artistic eye.

Shooting Techniques for Coastal Photography

Intentional Camera Movement (ICM)

Using a slow shutter speed (1/4 second to 1 second), deliberately move your camera to transform waves and sand textures into abstract expressions. Vary your movement patterns—horizontal, circular, or zigzag motions—to create distinctly different moods in your images.

Long Exposure

With a tripod and ND filter, set your shutter speed to 1 second or longer. This technique smooths water into a dreamy, ethereal quality. Even in daylight, an ND filter makes this achievable without waiting for low light.

Backlighting Silhouettes and Low-Angle Reflections

Position yourself to capture silhouettes when the sun is high, or shoot from a low angle to capture the sky reflected in wet sand. This mirror-like reflection effect creates striking visual impact.

Utilizing Sand Patterns and Texture

  • Low-angle perspective: Emphasizes sand ripples—soft and diffused in front-lit conditions, dramatically dimensional in backlighting
  • Telephoto lenses (85mm and longer): Compression effect isolates texture patterns
  • Wide-angle lenses (16–24mm): Emphasizes dune dimensionality and sky contrast

Lens Selection for Long Exposure

Focal Length Best For
Wide-angle (16–35mm) Capturing broad wind-driven sand movement; leveraging depth and foreground-background relationships
Standard (35–50mm) Natural perspective that presents wind movement clearly and simply
Telephoto (70–200mm) Isolating sand pattern segments; emphasizing contrast between moving and still areas

For daytime shooting, aim for ND1000 (10-stop equivalent) as a starting point. Use a tripod and remote release or self-timer to eliminate vibration. Shoot during golden hour—early morning or late evening—when directional light creates the most dimensional shadows.

Applying an Artistic Perspective to Everyday Subjects

Beyond coastal work, the principles of "changing perspective," "controlling light," and "abstracting forms" apply to ordinary subjects everywhere.

  • Puddle reflections: Use rainwater to create inverted, dreamlike worlds
  • Weathered metal and aged walls: Close-up framing reveals the texture and character of time's passage
  • Shadows and silhouettes: Capture long, directional shadows during golden hour as geometric patterns within your composition
  • Asphalt texture: Low angles, directional light, and post-rain reflections transform plain pavement into minimalist art

Monochrome Subjects

Subjects with strong light-and-shadow contrast, distinctive texture, and defined form truly excel in black and white.

  • Shadows and silhouettes (backlit figures and objects)
  • Asphalt and concrete texture (especially after rain)
  • Mist, smoke, and water vapor (enhanced dimensionality)
  • Deteriorating buildings and corroded metal (conveying the passage of time)
  • Power lines, towers, and bridges (geometric compositions highlighting linear beauty)

When shooting monochrome, focus on three critical elements: contrast between light and shadow, texture selection, and simplified, uncluttered composition. Without color, these three factors become the primary drivers of visual impact.

Summary

Coastal photography success comes from mastering core techniques—ICM, long exposure, and backlighting silhouettes—then adapting your lens choice and exposure based on time of day and terrain (sand patterns, dunes, or paved surfaces). Remember that shifting your perspective transforms any subject, even ordinary ones, into compelling work. This mindset is invaluable on days when travel to special locations isn't possible.
