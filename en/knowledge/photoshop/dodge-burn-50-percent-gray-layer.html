Dodge and Burn Retouching Fundamentals: Creating Even Light with a 50% Gray Layer

Master dodge and burn retouching using a 50% gray overlay layer. Learn brush settings, selection techniques, and Lab color methods to add even, natural lighting to portraits and landscapes without streaking.

Photoshop Updated: 2026-07-20

Ichiro Murata · Photographer / SideKick Developer

Dodge and Burn is a retouching technique that deliberately redesigns how light falls on an image by selectively brightening (dodging) or darkening (burning) specific areas. It's widely used for building dimension in portraits and emphasizing contrast in landscape photography.

Basic Method: The 50% Gray Overlay Layer

  1. Create a new layer (Shift+Ctrl+N) and select "50% Gray" from the fill options
  2. Set the layer mode to "Overlay" or "Soft Light" (Overlay produces a stronger effect)
  3. Select the Brush tool. Painting white brightens the image; black darkens it. A soft brush with 0% hardness and a flow rate of 5–10% is the most manageable setting
  4. Paint with light direction in mind. For portraits, brighten the cheekbones, bridge of the nose, forehead, and upper lip; darken the hollows of the cheeks, side of the nose, and jawline. For landscapes, this technique effectively emphasizes cloud edges and the edges of your subject
  5. Blend any unnatural areas with a subtle Gaussian Blur

Because this is non-destructive editing, you preserve the original image. You can also easily reduce the effect by lowering the layer's opacity. Colored dodge and burn—using warm or cool brushes instead of pure black and white—works well for correcting uneven skin tones.

Avoiding Brush Streaks

Painting directly with the brush can create patchy, unnatural results. Here are several ways to prevent this:

  • Lower flow significantly (3–5%)—reduce flow rather than opacity, and build up the effect gradually using brush pressure
  • Selection + blur—loosely select the area you want to retouch, then feather the edge (Feather 20–50px) before brushing to blend it smoothly with the surrounding image
  • Gradient tool instead of brush—using a radial gradient instead of direct brushwork creates more uniform results
  • Curves adjustment + mask—create a Curves adjustment layer and use the layer mask for selective application, making corrections easier since you're not painting directly
  • Frequency separation—separate the image into high frequency (texture) and low frequency (color and light) layers, then apply dodge and burn only to the mid-frequency band for shadow and highlight work that appears seamless and natural

Improving Precision: Luminosity-Based Selections

Creating selections from the original image's brightness allows you to target specific tonal ranges. This reduces unevenness and makes natural light adjustment easier.

  1. In the Channels panel, Ctrl+click the RGB (or blue) channel to create a selection from the bright areas
  2. To dodge the shadows instead, invert the selection (Ctrl+Shift+I)
  3. Feather the boundary (Feather 30–100px)
  4. Create a 50% gray overlay layer and brush within the selection (the selection prevents uneven coverage)

For even greater precision, use a luminosity mask on a Curves adjustment layer to target only the highlights or only the shadows.

Lab Color Mode Selection

Converting to Lab Color mode isolates the L channel (luminosity), allowing you to make more accurate brightness selections while preventing color casts.

  1. Go to "Image" > "Mode" > "Lab Color"
  2. In the Channels panel, select the L channel and create a selection from it
  3. Return to RGB mode and apply dodge and burn using that selection

Choosing Your Approach

  • Starting out: Reduce flow and paint, or use a selection with feather
  • Fine-tuning results: Curves + mask, or frequency separation
  • Eliminating overall unevenness: Gaussian Blur, or gradient tool

When using luminosity-based selections, always combine them with feathering or adjustment layers. Avoid applying the effect too strongly—excessive dodge and burn creates an over-processed HDR look. Control the strength with layer opacity.

Summary

Dodge and Burn is accessible once you master the "50% gray layer + brush" foundation, but achieving even, natural results requires additional refinement: flow adjustment, selections based on tonality, and frequency separation. Start with the basic technique, then when unevenness becomes a concern, combine it with luminosity-based selections.

Deciding where and how much light to add varies subtly from image to image—it's actually a domain that's difficult to automate.
